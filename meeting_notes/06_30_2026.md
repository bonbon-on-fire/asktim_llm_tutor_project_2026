### First round of simulated-conversation test results

- Ran 108 simulated conversations: 3 exercises + 3 practice problems × 18 student personas (chaotic / clueless / cooperative, 6 each) × 10 turns, tutor_05 on Claude, with a lecture-free context (course + syllabus + the problem).
- Graded all 108 with the Claude judge (judge_08 / rubric_08): mean 37.1/40, 0 failures.
- Scores by persona type: cooperative 39.4, chaotic 37.0, clueless 35.0 (out of 40). Exercises and practice problems score almost identically.
- Charts checked in under `visualization/outputs/sc2x/`.

### Where the tutor is weakest / fit for the course

- Weakest on dialogue quality with clueless students (~79%) and pedagogy with clueless students (~88%); communication is strong across all persona types (99–100%).
- Pattern from the lowest transcripts (e.g., chaotic on exercise 1, 31/40): the tutor correctly holds the line on academic integrity, but gets marked down for repetitive refusals without reframing and for not pivoting to productive tutoring sooner — not for being wrong.
- Important caveat for a quantitative course: every problem is an auto-graded numeric-entry or multiple-choice / select-all item (no free-response). The current rubric grades pedagogy / dialogue / communication, NOT whether the tutor's quantitative guidance reaches the correct numeric answer — so the judge scores can look strong while math correctness is still untested.
- A production bug surfaced during testing and was fixed: LaTeX / backslashes in an assignment (e.g., `\sum`) crashed the tutor's prompt injection; fixed in `tutor/run_tutor.py` and the Sandbox renderer.

### Next testing steps and deployment path

- Worth measuring RAG's value directly (RAG lecture context vs the lecture-free baseline) and testing how much context the tutor actually needs.
- Resolve the coverage gap (Verified access or wait for release) to test the later weeks and exams.
