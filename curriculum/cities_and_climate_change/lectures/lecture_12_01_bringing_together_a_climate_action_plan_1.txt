My name is Janelle Knox-Hayes and I'm
an Associate Professor in the Department of Urban Studies
and Planning at MIT.
This video is about bringing together a climate action plan.
As many climate action plans are developed
in segments and through a collaborative process,
bringing together a cohesive CAP requires a dedicated review
process with authors continually referencing
their original objectives to confirm
that all components of a CAP refer to
and address these objectives.
As C40 cities states, the CAP is the city's central vehicle
for building a narrative of urgency, opportunity,
and transformation, and to inform public, private,
and community stakeholders of the city's direction of travel.
To support this intention, a compiled CAP
should strive to be accessible to multiple stakeholders,
utilizing clear language, and reducing technical jargon,
integrating visualizations to explain complex concepts,
and diagram out strategies can support a CAP's
adoption across city government and within community
initiatives.
A collated climate action plan serves as a roadmap for cities
to address emissions objectives and tackle
related socioeconomic and equity-driven challenges
through a strategic process.
The CAP will come together with an implementation plan
and a monitoring and reporting framework
in order to demonstrate the CAP's success.
Metrics for measuring success should be included in the CAP
to clarify how actions for emissions reduction
will be assessed at specific intervals in the CAP's
implementation.
In recognition of a shift to trade-off analysis in addition
to or in order to replace cost benefit analyses,
a CAP may also include a trade-off analysis matrix
to support the assessment of mitigation and adaptation
strategies in addressing both emissions
reductions and socioeconomic and equity impacts.
