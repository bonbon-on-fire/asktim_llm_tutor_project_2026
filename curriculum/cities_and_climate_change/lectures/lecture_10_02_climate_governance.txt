
PROFESSOR: As we discussed earlier in this module,
cities are complex ecological systems, uniquely
endowed with attributes and functions that
enable them to respond to challenges like climate change.
But in order to do so, they have to articulate
vertical and horizontal networks and engage everyone
in urban climate action.
This means rescaling from governance systems that
have focused almost exclusively on the national level,
and shift downward in order to activate regional, municipal,
and neighborhood leadership.
This requires a careful articulation
of human and non-human entities, actors,
and initiatives that transcend typical jurisdictions
and boundaries, given the nature of urban ecosystems
and their particular vulnerability
to climate change.
Effective climate governance will also
require extending discussions and decision
making beyond government in a transdisciplinary dialogue that
brings together all segments of civil society.
This includes diverse types of expertise, embedded
in different cultural values, forms of knowledge,
and normative frameworks.
We must act quickly to negotiate conflicting land
uses, private interests, and local community priorities
to maximize public benefit.
But this cannot happen in a vacuum.
Therefore, in order to build equitable and just climate
action, we need everyone and their voices at the table.
In summary, urban climate governance
strengthens existing institutional capacities,
scientific and technical expertise,
supplementing this through the engagement
of multiple other actors and networks.
We need all hands on deck if we want our efforts
to create effective infrastructure improvements,
innovative policies, and comprehensive legislation that
addresses climate change.
