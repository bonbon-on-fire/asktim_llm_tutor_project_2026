PROFESSOR: Environmental justice is
a term that refers to the fair treatment
and meaningful involvement of all people
regardless of race, color, national origin, or income
in relation to the siting, implementation, and enforcement
of environmental and land use laws, regulations,
and policies.
It is specifically created to address
the disproportionate environmental burdens
that such decisions have had on low income
and communities of color.
Most importantly, environmental justice
is a social movement led by frontline communities who
live in neighborhoods that have been
subject to the injustices associated
with the exposure of noxious uses
and environmental contamination.
Stemming from environmental justice,
climate justice is a concept that
addresses the unbalanced distribution of climate change
burdens and the responsibilities that apply to address this,
showing that the communities most affected
are the least connected with the causes of climate change.
Both of these movements are rooted
on a definition of justice entailing at least
three important dimensions.
First, there is distributive justice
regarding the equitable distribution
of environmental burdens.
Second, we have procedural justice
associated with the equal treatment and respect
in decision making processes.
And third, there's corrective justice
associated with fairness and punishment with direct input
and oversight from those affected to guarantee
that damages are repaired.
True equitable climate action needs to seek justice,
and to do so all of these dimensions need to be met.
