Practice Problem 2: Cost Accounting at Youkea
Youkea is an upcoming Scandinavian office furniture retailer that is just establishing itself in the New England area. Youkea has specialized in office chairs. While they have three stores, their Boston branch is serving only business customers, and act as a combined warehouse and store for the Boston Area business customers.
To assess the profitability of different business customers at the Boston branch, Youkea has used a simple mark-up to allocate overhead costs to customers based on volume (number of chairs). This means that larger customers have been allocated a larger share of overhead costs. A manager at Youkea is now suspecting this to be misleading, since customers consume very different amounts of the resources that generate overhead costs.
One difference is in order handling. There are currently two ways for customers to order chairs from Youkea: through Youkea's webpage or over the phone. Orders that come in over the phone need to be entered manually into the system, line by line. As a result, the cost of handling a phone order depends on the number of order lines. Orders that come in through the webpage can be easily transferred into the system and only incurs a one-per-order order entry cost. Last year, Youkea processed 700 orders, for a combined total of 4,800 chairs. 20% of the orders were made over the phone, for a total of 800 order lines. The order entry costs associated with these phone orders represented 85% of total order entry costs.
Customers also differ in how the chairs are delivered to them. A customer may use the default option of common carrier delivery. For such deliveries, Youkea pays the carrier based on the number of chairs. For instance, delivering two chairs to a customer costs Youkea twice as much as delivering one chair. However, a customer may instead choose to receive express delivery using Youkea's own distribution truck, which is operated by Youkea personnel. The cost of these deliveries depends only on the number of shipments. That is, delivering one chair is just as costly to Youkea as delivering two chairs to the same customer. Last year, 100 such express deliveries were made by Youkea's own truck, delivering a total of 800 chairs.
All chairs that are delivered to customers from the Boston branch have arrived from a distribution center. Costs in the warehouse area of the branch tend to depend on the throughput.
Based on this, the manager believes that an activity based approach to assessing the cost of the customers at the Boston branch may be in place.
We assume that the Boston branch is operating at full capacity. From the income statement we have the following information:
Excerpt from Income Statement

Warehouse personnel	$ 400,000

Delivery personnel	$ 100,000
Other delivery expenses	$ 10,000
Order entry expenses	$ 20,000
Operating Income	$ 80,000

Practice Problem 4: Cost accounting at RCH Industries
Let us once again return to RCH Industries!
As you might remember from Week 2, RCH decided to re-plan the network after noticing the differences in demand for Stylish and Leisure items at different DCs. Since the two style groups were shipped in slightly different totes, transportation costs differed between the two style groups for each arc.
In this assignment we will focus on one of the DCs in the network - DC 4. As you may remember from before, the market served by DC4 has a total demand of 150 totes; 55 totes of Stylish and 95 totes of Leisure.
When an inbound shipment arrives at the DC, it is received by the warehousing personnel and the totes are temporarily stored in the DC. Totes carrying Stylish products cannot be stacked in the warehousing and storage process, so while 55 of the 150 totes that pass through the DC in a week are carrying stylish products, 80% of warehousing and storage personnel time is spent handling totes carrying stylish products, and 60% of equipment hours are used on those same totes.
When totes are ready for dispatch, customers can choose the type of distribution vehicles they wish for receiving the totes.  There are two types of vehicles requested by customers, EH-van (electric hybrid van) or LTL distribution truck. Some customers prefer EHV because it has a lower environmental impact and it is easier to schedule this type of delivery vehicle, giving the company more flexibility in receiving.  RCH's cost for the transportation is the same for either conveyance type, and is a function of the number of shipments made.  There are, however, different efficiencies associated with loading the totes on the different vehicles.
EH-vans handle 30 of the 150 totes shipped each week, but using the EH-Vans consumes 60% of all outbound shipment processing costs.  Using the EH-vans, the warehouse workers must load the totes manually, tote by tote, therefore costs are generally a function of the number of totes handled.
The remaining 120 totes are shipped using LTL transports. Shipments via LTL can be quickly loaded by forklift and the loading and processing costs are mainly driven by the number of shipments.
The 30 totes shipped via EH-van represent 5 of the 35 weekly shipments. RCH wishes to understand the cost-to-serve associated with providing customers these different delivery options.
The current operation appears to be working at full capacity.
RCH Industries have identified the following relevant activities:
Warehousing and storage Stylish
Warehousing and storage Leisure
Shipment processing and loading of EHV
Shipment processing and loading of LTL
Delivering totes
They have also identified the following relevant weekly costs:
Personnel, warehousing and storage: $800
Shipment processing and loading (personnel) : $600
Shipment processing and loading (non-personnel): $200
Facility and equipment: $200
Freight (total shipment costs): $1,000
