## Focus on the Problem

What are the important things to the supply chain, and what are the trivial things? All- each SKU to each pickup point to each ship-to point? Why not do everything all together? We are in the age of big data. Computers are very fast. Why not model everything? And I'll talk about why we can't in a second. But you're going to have to be making this decision on what do I include in the model, and how do I decide what to treat differently? So let me give you some tips on this. The first thing you can do is leverage what you've learned in previous lessons about segmentation. We talked about applying the power law-- remember the Pareto principle, the 80-20 rule. Goes by many names-- about different products and customers. Make sure you're focusing on those products and customers that are really making a difference to the supply chain. They're really driving the cost, driving the performance. You want to aggregate data as needed. I don't need to model each individual SKU. We want to combine them and customers in logical and smart ways. And we'll talk more about that in a second. You want to make sure that the level of detail that you include in the model makes sense for the decision at hand. ## Be Experimental
The third suggestion we have is to be experimental. And there's two aspects to this. The first one, we ask you to go spiral. What do I mean by this? Well, I suggest that you use the spiral method versus what's known as the waterfall method. The classic way of developing software, actually, was the waterfall method, where you spend six months understanding what the customer wants. Then you spend six months developing the requirements. Then you spend a year or six months, actually, writing the code. You test it, and then you deliver it. So it's like a waterfall-- each step falls to the other. The problem with that is when you get to the end, the requirements might have changed, or the customers didn't really quite understand what they were being asked. So as opposed to that, there's the spiral method that I'm showing up in the top right corner. And the idea is you start simple. You evaluate it. See how well it does. Get feedback, and incorporate that in the next version. So you start small, and you keep building and building and building. It's a great way to develop software, for a bunch of different reasons. And it's very similar to what we talked about back in SC1x, about the Pinata principle. Give people something to comment on fast. Be ugly early. Just give them something to critique because something you fix at this level early on will be much easier to fix than something out here after you've spent six months developing the model. So the beautiful thing about the spiral method is that start small, add to it, and it really improves the overall solution. So some of the benefits here-- you can build confidence in the model early because you get people on board. And if they see an early flaw, they can incorporate that into the next version very quickly. You can test the data quickly and identify outliers. This can help you separate them. This can help you separate the wheat from the chaff that we talked about earlier. And it can lead to an earlier answer. You might not need the full model. It might be that having something simple with just one product, for example, instead of trying to model each SKU, leads to an answer that's appropriate. And it also keeps those stakeholders engaged, so they're involved in the development and the creation of the model as well. So I encourage you to go spiral and use that spiral method. But I also encourage you, once the model's developed, just go crazy. The what-if scenarios are the best part of doing one of these engagements. So there's a big difference between creating a model and running a model. Creating a model is painful. It's time consuming. It can be very frustrating. Running a model is fast. It's fun. And it can be enlightening. So that's the reason for creating the model is to be able to run it. So what are the benefits? You can test and cost out all these different competing strategies and ideas. If someone has a strange or wacky idea, you can model it and see how well it compares and see, does it make sense? Because now you can model things and make decisions that are not pure cost based, but you can see what the cost implications would be if that strategy was followed. Additional runs provide insights into the internal trade-offs. You start understanding what level of service does to different flows within your network. That's the internal kind of trade-offs within your network. It provides opportunities for serendipitous discovery. What do I mean there? Something you weren't even looking for, but you see something because the way you're modeling it, it might make you look at your supply chain with fresh eyes and come up with something you hadn't thought of before. Multiple scenarios also better prepare your firm for future conditions. Try it under different settings, different input, and you see how the supply chain reacts. We did this when we looked at robust optimization. It's also a way to ride better communication with your stakeholders. Your stakeholders will probably want to see many different what-if scenarios. ## Separate the Math from the Decisions
The fourth and final suggestion that we have is to make sure you separate the math from the decisions. You need to remember that models don't make decisions. People do. The people are making the ultimate decisions. The math and the models are there to support us. So let's talk about some of these things. What is the mathematics and what are the models good for? Well, primarily, the models are awesome at making trade-offs between thousands of variables. Humans can't do this as well. But by setting up these mathematical models, we're able to make those trade-offs across the board. However, models will never be able to consider all the factors that are at play here. And models can never fully represent reality. Remember, they're caricatures. They're not photographs. Also, optimization models will do anything to save a penny, a dollar, a euro, a drachma, whatever. They will always look to minimize their objective function. And so it will do anything. And what seems like a great solution to the model might not make sense in reality. So for example, if you're selecting the top three distribution centers and the model picks three out, you should always look at the fourth, fifth, six, whatever choice to see if the difference is only a penny, or a couple dollars, where the other options would be better from a business sense. You should also always do sensitivity analysis- Do the models see what happens if I change the costs a little bit, or change the demands a little bit to see how fragile or robust the actual solutions are? Because optimization models, like we said, will do anything to reduce its objective function. This leads to the last two major points. The first is that models should be used for decision support-- decision support, not for the decision itself. These are decision support tools. So what comes out of the model is another piece of input for the decision maker to make, because these executives and managers of these supply chains, they have additional experience and insights into the overall situation that are just as valid as the mathematical model itself. So remember that these models are decision support tools. They're not making the decision itself. All right, so we went over four suggestions for you.
