## Centralized versus Decentralized
So let's dive just a little deeper into the centralized versus decentralized decision-making process. So there are pros and cons. And these are really two extreme solutions. For the centralized option, that's where-- here's my corporate headquarters, and let's say I have these different business units, or different divisions in different regions across the world. And the idea is that I will make all the decisions at that central location. And these could be deciding how much inventory to stock, what to procure, who to buy from for transportation services, all the different rules and policies. So the benefit of doing it up here at this central location is that we leverage economies of scale. I might be able-- for procurement, for example, be able to buy, get a high enough volume where I get a larger discount since I'm buying for one, two, three, four different regions in this case. It allows me to harmonize policies and practices so I can establish best practices, things that make sense for my industry, for my company and strategy. And I can roll those out and make sure that everyone is using, say, the same forecasting technique and being in parallel process for this. So everything is harmonized and standardized along the best practices as I deem fit for my entire company, and it also allows for global or optimal solutions. So I might be able to find a solution that is best for the entire company, not just for one or two of the individual divisions because I'm making the trade-off decisions centrally. Now, the other option here is just the opposite, where I don't make any decisions centrally. I make them all locally. This is decentralized. So the decision of who to procure from, how much inventory to stock, how to run my operations, demand planning, all of those things are done locally for my individual business unit or division.
