## Cost Accounting/Cost Systems
Hi. Welcome to the supply chain finance module. This segment will focus on cost accounting systems. Now, in this segment, the objective is not to make you an expert. Instead, I want to introduce you to some fundamental concepts relating to cost accounting systems, which are also referred to as cost systems. You'll learn that there are different methods used to track cost, and that there are some trade-offs and limitations associated with the different choices that are made for recording costs. Let's start with cost accounting. Cost accounting serves a different objective than financial accounting and different than tax accounting. The objective for cost accounting is to measure costs that will enable the firm to understand their business operations. And it enables the firm to make better decisions, informed with proper information about the costs associated with conducting their business. Cost accounting creates reports and conducts analysis for use by managers within the firm. The analyses are not expected to be used externally. They are used to inform and motivate management action. Therefore, the information that will best inform the management and provide decision support really ought to be tailored for each firm, depending on their situation and their objectives. Keep in mind that for cost accounting, the accountants do not have to follow the Generally Accepted Accounting Principles, because it's not important to make comparisons with external parties. Any comparison of product costs with external companies' product costs would first need to be studied to make sure that the measures are comparable. There are many different systems that exist to track costs and allocate overhead costs. The traditional cost system sets a standard for the cost and then compares the variance from that standard when assessing performance. They often use one factor to allocate the costs, the overhead cost. And that could be labor hours, machine time, or machine hours, or a variety of other different factors. Activity-based cost systems use cost drivers that are based on activities and the resources consumed in performing those activities in order to allocate overhead costs. Examples include the cost of monthly mortgage payment for the plant, and the cost of the monthly insurance bill. For these costs, it doesn't matter how many units you make. As an example, the cost of materials, well, that will vary depending on how many products you make. ## Activity Based Costing
Hi. Welcome to the Supply Chain Finance Module. This segment will focus on activity-based costing, also known ABC or ABC accounting. Our objective in this segment is to introduce you to the activity-based cost accounting, to review the principles and use of ABC, and make you aware of the ABC method. But we're not going to make you an ABC expert. That would require a lot more time and experience. Hopefully, you'll get enough so that you can be conversant on the issues and recognize the importance and the power of an activity-based system. The first question to ask is, why activity-based cost accounting? Well, traditional methods of allocating overhead worked for times where labor comprised a large part of the product cost. Using volume or the amount of labor hours used in a product as a proxy for the allocating the smaller overhead costs made sense. And it seemed to provide adequate product cost information for management. Today, things are different. Generally speaking, today labor represents a smaller percentage of the product cost. And material and overhead costs represent a higher percentage of the total product cost. In this situation, volume or labor hours would not serve as a good proxy for assessing the consumption of overhead costs. Therefore, it's important to recreate a system that provides a more accurate allocation of overhead to the product costs. Otherwise, management will have inaccurate assessments of the product cost, which may lead to poor product investment decisions. Let's go over a simple example. Assume a children's book company makes two products. One's a standard story book. And another product is the same story book that has been tailored in story and design to a specific child. The actual cost to physically produce the product would be approximately the same. But the customized book would require additional software, product design, additional content creation from writers, and would require a different distribution system to deliver the product to the child. In fact, the company offers free shipping for custom orders. So they're not charging for the additional cost to distribute the product. In the most recent month of operation, for example, the company produced and delivered 2,000 books. 1,900 of them were standard, and 100 were custom. Now the company, to offset the additional costs associated with the custom book, they have added $10 to the sales for a custom product. The overhead cost of distribution center operations are $2,740, although this includes some costs that are specific to standard books, such as the LTL shipping costs, and some costs that are specific to custom books, which would be the book prep and the direct shipment cost. Now using the information provided, if the company allocates the DC costs to each book based on volume, which would be a traditional measure, then the overhead cost per book is $2,740 divided by 2,000 units, or $1.37 per book. This spreads all of the costs evenly across each unit. But of course, as we know, the standard and custom books require different activities that have different costs. We see the custom books used $1,475 of DC activities specific for custom books and $65 in pooled activities, meaning that custom book activities consume $1,540 in resources. Now if we allocate those costs to the custom books, considering that there's 100 books, that results in a $15.40 per unit allocation for custom books. So that means the overhead, using an activity-based system, would be allocated at $15.40. Well, this is more than $14 higher than what would have been charged using a traditional system, or the $1.37 per book. Additionally, that $15.40 per unit allocation is, of course, more than $5 higher than what the company is recovering in cost through their $10 price increase. So this provides very misleading information. If the business leaders use the traditional allocations method, it's going to lead them to mistakenly believe that their custom book business is very profitable. Again, this is a very simple example to help illustrate how a change is necessary in many instances in order to provide more accurate information to business leaders. Well, as the example suggests, ABC is a cost accounting system that entails allocating overhead costs to cost objects based on the amount of resources that those cost objects consume. And the cost objects consume the resources in various activities. Now in activity-based costing, cost drivers are used to determine how much of the resources are consumed. Cost drivers take into consideration the activities that are involved in producing a product or service and the resources that are used in those activities. Ideally the cost driver would serve as a good proxy for the consumption and use of the resources in question. Now I've used the term cost object here. And I want to explain. A cost object can be a product, a service, a customer, or any other segmentation that a management team desires in order for them to better understand that particular situation. Again, keep in mind, this goes back to the concept that cost accounting systems are there to serve the internal needs and decision support needs for business leaders. In an ABC view, costs come from activities. To understand ABC, consider that any product is going to be created using a variety of activities. Therefore the total cost of producing that product would be the total cost of all the activities involved. Put another way, each product that the firm creates and offers is considered in terms of a bundle of activities. Now ABC is especially useful when there's a lot of variety and complexity associated with the production of products. This is the case because with ABC, one can identify costs coming from specific activities that offer a level of detail that the traditional methods of allocating them on the basis of labor hours or machine hours or volume just doesn't permit. When dealing with complexity, I would argue that more detail will provide more accuracy, which will enable better decisions. As a result of this improved accuracy available using ABC, activity-based costing has become one of the more important methods for getting accurate information to support business leaders in their product and service decisions. Let's follow the flow using an activity-based cost system from resources-- you see here, think about the indirect costs that represent the resources that are consumed-- to the activities-- we see four activities outlined here-- to the products, services, and customers. In this case, the cost object we're thinking of as a series of three products, but it could be, as we suggest before, services or customer segments. Now look, if you go online, you can find lots of examples in a variety of different ways to characterize the ABC flow. But they're all going to basically suggest this general flow. This is a simple one. Of course, it's not the only one. So what are the basic steps to ABC? As I had mentioned on the previous slide, if you go online, you can find many examples or a variety of steps for ABC. This is just one set. But you'll find that they are fairly similar. Let's walk through each of these briefly. The first step is to identify all the relevant activities. Usually the cost to describe this is coming from specific functions-- manufacturing, distribution, logistics, order management, order service, et cetera and so forth. But with activity-based costing, functions are not generally used. The activity represents a collection of tasks that sometimes include activities from or tasks from different functions. We describe the activities with a specific format. We describe them in terms of a verb followed by a direct object. This should be describing how the work is done, not necessarily by function or department. Now the second step is identifying the resources consumed for performing the activities. This is done through interview and observation. And it can get tricky because some resources are used in multiple activities. Note that when we're doing an activity-based cost analysis, we do not include unused capacity. The ABC method allocates the costs that are actually used in the production and delivery of the product. Now if the company elects to have excess capacity, that excess capacity is not considered a relevant cost that ought to be borne by a product. Otherwise the product cost would be artificially inflated because they would be reflecting the cost of providing the product and service and the cost of carrying the excess capacity. Those require different analyses. In step three, we start with the resources that were used, which were identified in step two. Now you know the resources used, identify the costs for those resources and then collect those into cost pools by activity. The next step is to determine the cost drivers. Really, what we're looking for is the best measure of the output of an activity, a single measure. So for example, if the activity is ship product, perhaps the best measure would be the number of orders shipped. Or if the activity is store packages, maybe the best measure would be the number of packages stored. But it could be the number of storage runs made. So it really depends on the situation. From our example, we see that the cost drivers are the number of tests associated with the test raw materials activity. Or the cost driver is the number of boxes when we're looking at packaging materials. Or the number of CAD hours when we're looking at design layout. Or the number of items shipped when you're looking at the ship items activity. In step five, we calculate the cost driver rate. This is very simple. We take the costs that were associated with the activity, and we divide it by the cost driver. From our example, we would divide the cost of test raw materials activity and divide it by the cost driver, which we know to be the number of tests. So if the test raw material activity generated $300 in costs, and there were 50 tests conducted, then the cost driver rate would be $300 divided by the cost driver of 50 tests for a cost driver rate of $6 per test. The last step is to trace the costs to the cost objects using the cost driver rates that we just identified. What we really want to do is identify how much of each activity is consumed by a particular product. So let's look at Product C from our example and determine how much of the overhead costs should be allocated to Product C. From the graphic, we can observe that Product C uses only two activities, package materials and ship items. So we see only two of those particular activities associated with Product C. Considering the facts here-- there are 50 boxes of Product C and there are five shipments of Product C-- we can calculate the cost driver rates to be $3 per box and $7 per item shipped. Therefore the total cost to allocate to Product C would equal the sum of 50 boxes times $3 per box and five shipments times $7 per item shipped for a total of $185. This approach would allow us to get a better assessment of cost for each of these different products based on the activities that they were used to create that service. Product A uses all four activities, so its cost allocation may be higher than the allocation for Product C that uses only two activities, unless the volumes for Product C are much higher than that for Product A. It really depends on the amount of use and the cost driver rates. Let's talk about key points. Hopefully, this has illustrated how an activity-based cost system can provide much more accuracy in the allocation of indirect costs especially in environments with a lot of complexity and large expenses, where there's lots of different indirect and support resources, with multiple products and customers or custom requirements. The system is designed to capture great detail. But of course, it does require additional effort and data. Activity-based cost systems are less important when a product cost has a high labor component or when it's a relatively simple environment where there's a single product or a limited diversity of products or customers or processes or geographies. ABC systems can provide a lot of detail to management that enables them to make product or customer investment decisions with the necessary accuracy. But an important question to ask is, how accurate does our cost reporting system need to be? It's a challenging question that is up for the management to decide. ## Working Capital
Hi. Welcome to the Supply Chain Finance module. In this segment, we will focus on working capital. Our objective in this segment is to introduce you to working capital and identify the role that supply chain managers play in assisting in the managing of working capital. First, what is working capital. Well qualitatively, working capital is a basic measure of both the company's efficiency as well as its short term financial health. One way to think about it is that it's the amount of financial resources that are used to conduct daily business. And it includes current assets such as accounts receivable, cash, and inventory. Tracking working capital is therefore important to best understand how the company's resources are being used to serve its business objectives. Now that is rather general, so a more detailed description is wanted. Quantitatively, we calculate working capital by subtracting the current liabilities from the current assets. More often than not, working capital is presented in terms of a positive monetary figure. When the figure is positive, it represents a net amount of financial resources or capital that's required in order to operate the business. And it also indicates that the business has enough current assets to continue operations. If the working capital figure is negative, that could be a good or a bad indicator. In the worst case, a negative working capital figure indicates that the business does not have enough current assets on hand to pay their accounts payable and short term debt obligations. If the company cannot convert other assets into cash, then the business will not be able to satisfy their short term obligations and they run the risk of losing credit or worse, they run the risk of bankruptcy. Now in some cases, a company is able to operate on negative working capital. How can that be. Well this can occur when a company can collect payments in advance of the time that they need to pay their suppliers. Meaning that they're using the money from customers to fund the operation of the business. This can work for subscription businesses or businesses where the customer pays upfront for a product that is produced and delivered at a later date. Now the primary components of working capital are accounts receivable, inventory, accounts payable cash, short term investments, and short term debt. Now we managers and leaders in the supply chain can have an impact on these factors. That is-- accounts receivable, inventory, and accounts payable. But not so much on these factors, short term investments and short term debt. And we'll see over the course of our time together what managers might be able to do to deal with accounts receivable, inventory, and accounts payable. Sometimes working capital is measured in terms of a ratio. And one of the more common ones is called the current ratio. The current ratio is calculated by dividing the current assets by the current liabilities. If the current ratio is less than one, the business has negative working capital, meaning that the current assets are less than the current liabilities. And that means that the company cannot pay their current liabilities with their current assets. If the current ratio is greater than one, but less than two, this is often considered a healthy range for the current ratio. And if the current ratio is above two, this is considered a potentially unhealthy range, possibly suggesting that the business is using its working capital inefficiently. In this case, the business may have too much inventory on hand compared with what is necessary to serve the business. This, of course, depends on the business model and go to market approach. So the ratio is more of an indicator that warrants deeper analysis. Now a more stringent ratio is called the acid test. The acid test uses a slightly different formula for calculating the current ratio, including only cash, accounts receivable, and short term investments in the numerator. It does not include inventory. This essentially assesses the ability of the company to pay off its current liabilities by using only current assets that are cash or that can be quickly converted into cash. Because it would likely take some time to convert inventory into cash, inventory is not included as asset in the acid test. Working capital performance is also measured in terms of working capital turnover. This is another ratio which gives an indication of how the company is able to generate sales using the working capital. This ratio helps answer the question, how effectively is a company using its working capital to generate sales. We calculate working capital turnover by dividing the sales for a particular period of time by the working capital. In general, a higher working capital turnover is preferable because it means that the company is able to generate more sales with the same amount of working capital. We've established that working capital is important for the business for several reasons. Of course, it's an indicator of the efficiency of the business, but also an indicator of the financial health of a business. Basically, can they pay their bills. Well given that, let's go over some options for the company to consider for actively managing their working capital. One option is to collect the receivables quickly. This may seem like an obvious pursuit, but it carries additional weight when put in the context of proactively managing the working capital. Some firms are very aggressive in collections and manage them by creating terms and conditions to provide incentives for their customers to pay them on time and even early. It's not uncommon to hear of a term called 2% net 10 days. And that means that the company would give a 2% discount on the outstanding balance if the customer pays your bill within 10 days of receipt of the invoice. Another way to manage the working capital is to stretch the payables out as long as possible. But there is a risk to taking this approach because this could damage the relationship with suppliers. It may also affect the suppliers willingness to supply and their ability to respond to critical needs. And worse, it may damage their supplier's ability to operate. So if an organization intends to extend the payable cycle with its suppliers, it may be better to structure those arrangements in advance with the supplier so that the supplier is not surprised and put into a potentially problematic flow situation. Companies can also manage the inventories to lower levels, so long as it isn't enough to provide the service that the business needs to serve their customers. Avoid cutting inventory to reduce working capital requirements without first assessing the impact of those cuts on service level. Another alternative is to consider converting current liabilities into long term liabilities. Now this doesn't make those liabilities go away, but it does take the obligation out of the current time frame. The supply chain manager has the potential to impact a number of the components of working capital. And the first and most significant impact will come from managing the inventory, just as we've mentioned. As I just suggested, managing the inventory levels down makes sense but only in the proper context of the business situation and the business objective, as well as the supply chain strategy. For example, if a company competes on the basis of having a high service level, then it makes sense for the company to maintain high inventory levels as well as responsive supply chains. All the same, the supply chain manager is responsible for making sure that the inventory policy is consistent with the business date and for managing the inventory levels efficiently and effectively. The supply chain manager also has an impact on two other primary components. And those are accounts receivable and accounts payable. By working with suppliers and customers, maintaining working relationships with each, and by structuring the terms and conditions of sale with each, the supply chain manager can directly affect the cash flows coming from customers and flowing to suppliers. This slide shows a few selected ways that supply chain managers can impact working capital through different supply chain practices and policies, some of which we've just described. On the left hand side of the slide, we see a list of practices or potential actions. The column on the right list advantages and then disadvantages from using that practice or policy. In the first example, we see extended payment terms from suppliers. This means that the company would arrange to pay the suppliers longer than the standard payment term. That helps working capital by reducing the amount of cash required to pay the supplier in the near term. But the supplier will probably increase their pricing at some point in order to offset the higher cost that they have to serve your company. On the second line we see discount for early payment by customers. This helps working capital by increasing the cash component and reducing the accounts payable component. But the company will receive less than the full revenue from the sale. If the company finances raw material or work in process for suppliers, this will raise the working capital requirements because the company's likely financing the materials with short term debt. On the plus side, it will more likely assure supply from that small supplier. So we can see from some of these examples that there is an impact on working capital as well as an impact on operating business from these different practices. Well that makes sense. Business operations will impact working capital and vice versa. So it is helpful to think about these in advance and select the practices that work best for the company and the financing plans. Let's talk about working capital requirements. How much working capital is necessary to properly operate the business. Well, enough working capital is enough to serve the customers, to deal with some variation in cash flows, and to operate the business overall. Keep in mind that when we say operate the business, this means operating at the target service level and therefore maintaining the requisite inventory to deliver that service level. The company should be considerate of variations in cash flows and therefore one should understand the timing of the operational cash flows. Start from the time of purchasing materials that the company would use to produce and sell the product, consider the time from the purchase of materials to the time of payment to the suppliers, and then consider the time from the product sale to the time it takes for the customer to pay for the product. These three time-driven development are components of the cash cycle, which we will speak about in another segment. Let's talk about the key points. Working capital represents the resources that are utilized to operate the business and keep it functioning. Positive working capital is generally better than negative working capital, but it really depends on the business model of the company and of the timing of the cash flows to suppliers and from customers. Remember, it's possible to operate with negative working capital. ## Cash-to-Cash Cycle
Hi. Welcome to the supply chain finance module. In this segment, we will focus on the cash to cash cycle, also known as the cash conversion cycle. Our objective for this segment is to introduce you to the concept of the cash to cash cycle. I'll also explain the mechanics of how to calculate the cash to cash cycle, and we'll review some of the ways to interpret that cycle. The cash to cash cycle is known by several terms, including the cash conversion cycle, the cash cycle, and the operating cycle. It is a liquidity measure, meaning that it provides an indication of the ability of the company to pay its bills using the cash, or other assets that can be quickly converted into cash. The cash cycle expresses liquidity in terms of the amount of time, measured in days, that a company uses to sell its inventory, collect its receivables, and pay its accounts payable. In simplest terms, the cash conversion cycle is the amount of time that it takes a company to use cash to buy materials, make a product, sell the product, and then get paid by the customer. This is how much time the company's cash is tied up in the operation of the business. The shorter the total cash cycle, the less amount of time that cash is needed. This is a good thing, because that means that the company can convert its assets into cash more quickly, making it more capable of meeting its financial obligations. As I just mentioned, the cash conversion cycle considers the time it takes for three specific cash cycles. First, consider the days of inventory outstanding. This is the number of days that it takes for the company to convert inventory into a sale. Next is the days of sales outstanding. This is the number of days that it takes the company to get paid by the customer. Lastly is the days of payables outstanding. This is the number of days that it takes the company to pay its suppliers. We calculate the cash conversion cycle by adding the days of inventory outstanding to the days of sales outstanding, and then subtract in the days of payables outstanding. Basically, we add the amount of time that we need to use the money-- making inventory, selling the product, and waiting to get paid-- and then we deduct the amount of time we get to use suppliers' money-- that is, the accounts payable. Let's look at each of these elements to fully understand each. The days of inventory outstanding is calculated by dividing the average inventory by the cost of sales-- also known as cost of goods sold, or COGS-- and then multiplying that by 365. The DIO answers the question, how long does it take the company to convert the inventory into a sale. In the formula we use cost of sales, or COGS, because inventory is maintained on the assets at cost. And the cost of sales indicates how much was sold over the year.
